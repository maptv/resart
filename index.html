1 Abstract

The circadian rhythm is a fundamental biological process that coordinates physiological and behavioral activities with the 24-hour light–dark cycle. This manuscript explores the temporal interplay among cortisol, melatonin, and serotonin, focusing on their synchronized fluctuations across the diurnal cycle and their collective impact on sleep, stress response, and mood regulation.

2 Introduction

Circadian rhythms orchestrate physiological processes via central and peripheral clocks, largely governed by the suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) in the hypothalamus. This master pacemaker integrates photic signals and synchronizes hormonal release patterns across multiple systems (Reppert and Weaver 2002; Hastings et al. 2018).

The hormones cortisol, melatonin, and serotonin exhibit distinct yet interdependent circadian profiles. Understanding their interplay is critical for elucidating mood disorders, sleep disturbances, and stress dysregulation.

3 Cortisol and the Diurnal Stress Axis

Cortisol, a glucocorticoid released via the hypothalamic–pituitary–adrenal (HPA) axis, shows a well-characterized daily rhythm. Levels peak shortly after awakening — the Cortisol Awakening Response (CAR) — and decline steadily throughout the day (Kirschbaum and Hellhammer 1999). This pattern aligns with metabolic activation and the preparation of the organism for daytime activity.

Chronic disruption of this rhythm, such as in shift work or major depression, leads to elevated or blunted cortisol rhythms, contributing to metabolic and psychological dysfunction (Miller et al. 2007).

4 Melatonin as a Timekeeper

Melatonin secretion from the pineal gland begins after dusk and peaks during the night, serving as a biochemical signal of darkness (Arendt 2005). Light exposure, particularly at blue wavelengths, inhibits melatonin production through retinal signaling to the SCN.

This nocturnal signal acts as a “hormonal clock,” coordinating peripheral rhythms and regulating sleep onset and maintenance.

5 Serotonin and Daytime Arousal

Serotonin, synthesized from tryptophan in the raphe nuclei, displays elevated concentrations during wakefulness and reduced activity during sleep (Jacobs and Azmitia 1991). It forms a biochemical link with melatonin, as melatonin synthesis depends on serotonin availability and enzymatic conversion within the pineal gland (Axelrod and Weissbach 1970).

Dysregulation in serotonergic tone disrupts mood and contributes to circadian misalignment in depressive disorders.

6 Interactions and Integration

The three hormones demonstrate a coordinated feedback system:

  • Cortisol promotes arousal and mobilizes energy during daylight.
  • Serotonin regulates mood and maintains daytime wakefulness.
  • Melatonin signals sleep onset and circadian phase resetting.

Environmental cues, particularly light exposure and social timing, entrain this system. Misalignment between internal and external rhythms leads to circadian desynchrony, common in jet lag, shift work, or mood disorders (Mistlberger and Antle 2011).

7 Conclusion

Cortisol, melatonin, and serotonin form a dynamic endocrine triad that reflects and reinforces circadian homeostasis. Understanding these relationships provides insights into therapeutic interventions for sleep and affective disorders.
